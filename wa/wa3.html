Introduction

Art in Europe, also known as Western Art, covers the history of visual artistic expression across the continent. This website showcases art from Ancient Greek and Roman art to Modern artistic movements, though some periods are omitted for brevity.

Ancient

Venus de Milo

Ancient art encompasses three main societies. The Minoan civilization, known as the oldest civilization of Europe, produced artwork with a variety of subject matter and exceptional craftsmanship. Classical Greek and Roman art are more widely recognized. Ancient Greece is renowned for its marble sculptures and architecture. Roman art, heavily influenced by Greek art, often depicted their gods and the upper class.

Medieval

Medieval Painting

Medieval art, often funded by the Church, is predominantly religious in focus. It is characterized by a lack of realism, particularly evident in the depiction of babies. This stylistic approach arose because the techniques of perspective and realistic human figures were lost after the fall of Rome and were not reinvented until the Renaissance.

Renaissance

Birth of Venus

The Renaissance is characterized by a renewed focus on the arts of Ancient Greece and Rome, leading to significant evolution in style, technique, and subject matter. This movement began in Italy, due to its Roman heritage, and spread throughout Europe. The Renaissance remains influential to this day.

Mannerism, Baroque, and Rococo

The Swing

After the Renaissance, European art split into two movements: Mannerism and Baroque. Mannerism is noted for its imaginative quality and realism, almost to the point of perfection. The Baroque movement, building on Renaissance principles, introduced more movement, dramatic lighting, and intense drama. This evolved into the Rococo movement, characterized by pastel palettes and a sense of whimsy.

Modern Art

Starry Night

Modern art in Europe encompasses various movements: Impressionism, Post-Impressionism, Fauvism, Cubism, Modern Art, Modernism, and Late Modernism. Each movement built upon the lessons of prior movements to create something entirely new. For example, Impressionists are known for their emphasis on light and bold brushstrokes.
